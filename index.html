This site is a collection of my work throughout my time at San Diego State University, organized by semester. As the semesters progress, so does the way I approach design. What began as a general interest in art slowly turned into something more intentional, whether that was through digital work, hands-on projects, or extrcurricular experiences.

This site is not a portfolio to showcase finished work, but about growth, shifts in perspective, and figuring out what kind of work feels meaningful to me. Thank you being here :).

Year 2 - 02

Year 2 - 02

01/17/2024 - 05/09/2024

  • ART 103 Three Dimensional Design
  • ART 241 Graphic Design I
  • ART 242 Typography I
  • GEOL 303 Natural Disasters

This was my first semester really exploring graphic design. In this project, I focused on how typography can shape a design beyond just readability. I started to see type as form, pattern, and space, not just letters. It changed the way I approach layouts and made me more intentional about how things are arranged on a page.

This was a group project where we designed and built a giant inflatable heart-eye emoji. I loved working on this because it pushed me out of the digital space and into something more hands-on. It taught me a lot about collaboration, like how to balance different ideas and actually listen to others. We also had to test everything in smaller versions before building it full scale, which showed me how important planning and iteration are. It was something I had never even thought about designing before, and it made me realize I enjoy working physically, not just digitally.

This project involved carving abstract and symbolic forms into clay tiles. Working with a soft, malleable material like clay was really satisfying, and it was one of the moments where I realized how much I enjoy making things with my hands. The process of carving and shaping felt very different from digital work and helped me think more physically about form and texture.

This is one of my favorite classes I’ve taken at SDSU. I learned how to use tools like a blowtorch and designed these handmade metal earrings with organic curves and a patina finish. I wanted to focus on femininity through softer, flowing shapes. Being able to physically make something from start to finish was really rewarding, and even more so when these ended up selling for $20.

This is from our final critique where we designed and made necklaces with three charms. I felt really grateful to be surrounded by such talented artists and craftspeople. Seeing everyone’s work pushed me and made the experience feel really collaborative and inspiring.

This was my final project for Digital Photography I. The assignment was to create a series, and I chose to photograph trolley tracks throughout San Diego. I focused on how the surrounding environment changes and adapts around the structure of the tracks and stations.

This was a deeply personal project where I created logo iterations for my mom’s small business. I wanted the design to feel clean and professional while still holding a sense of warmth and personalization. It was meaningful to design something that directly connects to my family.

During this internship with the San Diego County Sheriff’s Office, I got to work in a really active and collaborative environment. I especially appreciated my supervisor, Gumaro Peña, and the relationships I built with the team. Being there made me realize I value connection and impact in a more personal way. It shifted how I think about design, from just organizing information to creating something more intentional. That realization was one of the most important things I took away from the experience.

I was commissioned by Commander Heyman of the 75th Air Force ROTC detachment to design his final commander’s coin before retiring. This project felt very different from anything I had done before. What stood out most to me was how much he genuinely appreciated the design, which made the experience really meaningful.

This is my boss, who became a big influence on how I think about work. He’s a retired amputee horticulturalist, and working with him showed me what it means to make a direct impact on someone’s life. Even though the work isn’t design-focused, it brought me a lot of joy because I could see the impact of what I was doing in real time.

While working as a personal assistant to an interior designer and architect, I’ve been involved in creating custom tiles from scratch. This process includes shaping clay, trimming, drying, glazing, and firing. These tiles are being made for a kitchen island and will be installed soon. It’s been a really hands-on experience and showed me how design can exist fully outside of a digital space.

This project started with drawing an orthographic view of a sea otter for a 3D modeling assignment. We then transformed it into a more monstrous toy design by adding and removing characteristics in Maya. It helped me understand how 2D drawings translate into 3D forms and how small changes can completely shift a character’s personality.
